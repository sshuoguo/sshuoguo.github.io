Understanding Adam from a Vector Field Perspective

1. Question

Adam is one of the most widely used optimization methods in modern machine learning, yet its behavior is often difficult to interpret theoretically.

What does Adam actually do to the optimization landscape? Can it be understood as inducing a different vector field compared to standard gradient descent?

2. Gradient Descent as a Vector Field

Standard gradient descent follows the update rule:

x_{k+1} = x_k - η ∇f(x_k)

This can be interpreted as a discretization of the continuous-time system:

dx/dt = -∇f(x)

which defines a vector field over the space. At each point x, the vector -∇f(x) determines the direction of motion.

3. What Adam Changes

Adam modifies this vector field by introducing coordinate-wise scaling based on past gradients.

Instead of following the true gradient field, Adam effectively follows:

v(x) = -D(x) ∇f(x)

where D(x) is a diagonal matrix determined by second-moment estimates.

This means that Adam does not follow the original gradient field, but a distorted version of it.

4. Geometric Interpretation

From a geometric perspective, this transformation can be viewed as a local rescaling of the space.

Coordinates with large historical gradients are suppressed, while those with small gradients are amplified.

This creates an anisotropic geometry, where the notion of "steepest descent" is altered.

5. Implications for Stability

This distortion may break desirable properties of the original gradient flow.

In particular, the modified vector field may no longer align with descent directions of the original objective.

This provides one possible explanation for the instability or divergence of Adam observed in some settings.

6. Open Questions

This perspective seems closely related to conditioning and preconditioning methods, but the exact relationship is not yet fully clear.

Can the geometry induced by Adam be characterized more formally? How does this affect convergence in high-dimensional problems?

Understanding these questions may help clarify when and why adaptive methods succeed.
